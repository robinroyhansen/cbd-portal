# CBD Safety Profile: What Research Shows

By Robin Roy Krigslund-Hansen | 12+ years in CBD industry
Last updated: January 21, 2026

---

## Quick Summary

CBD has a favorable safety profile with most studies showing mild side effects at typical doses. The WHO concluded CBD is "generally well tolerated with a good safety profile." Common side effects include fatigue, diarrhea, and appetite changes. Significant concerns include drug interactions and liver enzyme elevation at high doses. Long-term safety data remains limited.

---

## What Major Organizations Say

### World Health Organization (2018)
> "CBD is generally well tolerated with a good safety profile. Reported adverse effects may be as a result of drug-drug interactions between CBD and patients' existing medications."

### FDA (Epidiolex Approval)
Identified CBD as safe enough for prescription medication status, with manageable side effects at therapeutic doses.

### European Food Safety Authority
Ongoing safety assessment; currently considers CBD a "novel food" requiring more data.

## Common Side Effects

Based on clinical trial data and post-market surveillance:

| Side Effect | Frequency | Notes |
|-------------|-----------|-------|
| Fatigue/drowsiness | Common (10-20%) | Usually mild, may decrease over time |
| Diarrhea | Common (10-20%) | Often dose-related |
| Appetite changes | Common | Decrease or increase possible |
| Dry mouth | Occasional | Similar to other cannabinoids |
| Dizziness | Occasional | More common at higher doses |
| Nausea | Occasional | Usually mild |
| Weight changes | Variable | Can go either direction |

Most side effects are dose-dependent and often resolve with continued use or dose adjustment.

## Serious Concerns

### Liver Enzyme Elevation
- Seen primarily at high doses (>300mg/day)
- Epidiolex trials showed elevated ALT/AST in some patients
- Usually reversible when CBD stopped
- Monitoring recommended at pharmaceutical doses
- Less concerning at typical supplement doses

### Drug Interactions
- CBD inhibits CYP3A4 and CYP2C19 enzymes
- Can increase levels of certain medications
- Particularly relevant for:
  - Clobazam (seizure medication)
  - Warfarin (blood thinner)
  - Some immunosuppressants
  - Various psychiatric medications

### Quality and Contamination
- Unregulated products may contain:
  - More or less CBD than labeled
  - THC above legal limits
  - Heavy metals
  - Pesticides
  - Solvents
- Third-party testing essential

## Who Should Be Cautious

### Pregnancy and Breastfeeding
- Insufficient safety data
- FDA advises against use
- Potential developmental concerns
- CBD transfers to breast milk
- Better alternatives exist for most conditions

### Children
- Limited safety data outside epilepsy
- Epidiolex approved for certain seizure disorders
- General supplementation not recommended
- Developing brains may be more sensitive

### Liver Disease
- CBD metabolized by liver
- Higher risk of enzyme elevation
- Medical supervision recommended
- May need dose adjustment

### People Taking Medications
- Drug interaction potential
- Consult healthcare provider
- May affect medication levels
- Monitoring may be needed

---

## Toxicity and Overdose

### Acute Toxicity
- No fatal overdoses reported in humans
- LD50 not established in humans
- Very high doses may cause significant sedation
- Animal studies show high doses required for toxicity

### Comparison
CBD appears safer than many common substances:
- Alcohol: Toxic dose relatively close to intoxicating dose
- Acetaminophen: Liver toxicity at 2-3x recommended dose
- CBD: No established toxic dose at practical levels

---

## Long-Term Safety Considerations

### What We Know
- Generally well-tolerated in trials up to 2 years
- No evidence of withdrawal syndrome
- No evidence of physical dependence
- Cognitive effects appear minimal

### What We Don't Know
- Very long-term effects (10+ years)
- Effects of chronic use on specific populations
- Cumulative effects on liver function
- Optimal chronic dosing

---

## Safety vs. Quality Issues

Many reported problems stem from product quality, not CBD itself:

| Problem | CBD Issue? | Quality Issue? |
|---------|------------|----------------|
| Failed drug test | Possibly | Usually (THC contamination) |
| Unexpected high | No | Yes (THC presence) |
| Severe reaction | Rarely | Often (contaminants) |
| No effect | Possible | Often (underdosed) |

## Comparing to Alternatives

For conditions CBD is commonly used for:

| Substance | Addiction Risk | Organ Damage Risk | Overdose Risk |
|-----------|----------------|-------------------|---------------|
| CBD | Very low | Low | Very low |
| NSAIDs | None | Moderate (GI, kidney) | Moderate |
| Opioids | High | Low | High |
| Benzodiazepines | Moderate-high | Low | Moderate |
| Alcohol | Moderate | High | Moderate |

## Best Practices for Safety

1. **Start low, go slow** - Begin with low doses and increase gradually
2. **Use tested products** - Only buy from companies with third-party COAs
3. **Check for interactions** - Review medications with healthcare provider
4. **Monitor yourself** - Track effects and any adverse reactions
5. **Choose reputable sources** - Quality varies enormously
6. **Store properly** - Keep away from light and heat

---

## My Take

After 12+ years of observing CBD use, I consider it one of the safer wellness products available—when quality is ensured. The side effect profile compares favorably to most over-the-counter medications, and serious adverse events are rare.

That said, I don't dismiss safety concerns. Drug interactions are real and require attention. High-dose, long-term use hasn't been thoroughly studied. And the unregulated market means quality problems can create risks that CBD itself wouldn't cause.

My practical advice: CBD is generally safe for most adults when sourced properly and used responsibly. But treat it as you would any bioactive substance—with appropriate respect and awareness.
