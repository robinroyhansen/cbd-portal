# ISO Certification for CBD: What It Means

By Robin Roy Krigslund-Hansen | 12+ years in CBD industry
Last updated: January 21, 2026

---

## Quick Summary

ISO certification indicates that a CBD company or testing laboratory meets international standards for quality management, testing accuracy, or food safety. Common relevant certifications include ISO 17025 (lab accreditation), ISO 9001 (quality management), and ISO 22000 (food safety). While not required, ISO certification demonstrates commitment to quality and provides third-party verification of standards.

---

## Key Takeaways

- ISO = International Organisation for Standardisation
- Shows adherence to international standards
- ISO 17025 for testing labs is most relevant
- ISO 9001 for company quality management
- Not mandatory but indicates commitment

### The Organisation

**International Organisation for Standardisation:**
- Non-governmental body
- Develops international standards
- 167 member countries
- Covers many industries

### What ISO Standards Do

**Purpose:**
- Ensure quality
- Enable consistency
- Provide verification framework
- Create trust

## ISO Certifications Relevant to CBD

### ISO 17025: Laboratory Accreditation

**What It Is:**
- Accreditation for testing laboratories
- Verifies competence and accuracy
- International recognition
- Gold standard for labs

**Why It Matters for CBD:**
- Labs testing CBD should have this
- Ensures accurate results
- Verifies proper procedures
- Trustworthy testing

**What It Covers:**
- Technical competence
- Impartiality and consistency
- Equipment calibration
- Quality management

### ISO 9001: Quality Management

**What It Is:**
- Quality management system standard
- Company-wide quality processes
- Customer focus
- Continuous improvement

**Why It Matters for CBD:**
- Shows systematic quality approach
- Documented procedures
- Process controls
- Management commitment

**What It Covers:**
- Quality objectives
- Process documentation
- Resource management
- Performance monitoring

### ISO 22000: Food Safety

**What It Is:**
- Food safety management standard
- Applies to food chain organisations
- Includes supplements
- HACCP principles

**Why It Matters for CBD:**
- CBD products are ingestible
- Food safety relevant
- Contamination prevention
- Safe handling procedures

**What It Covers:**
- Hazard analysis
- Critical control points
- Traceability
- Emergency procedures

## ISO vs. GMP

### Comparison

| Factor | ISO | GMP |
|--------|-----|-----|
| Scope | Varies by standard | Manufacturing |
| Focus | Management systems | Production practices |
| Certification | Third-party | Often regulatory |
| Relevance | Labs & companies | Manufacturers |

### How They Complement

- ISO: Organisation-wide systems
- GMP: Manufacturing specifics
- Both together: Comprehensive quality
- Neither replaces the other

## What ISO Certification Means

### For Testing Labs (ISO 17025)

**If a Lab Has ISO 17025:**
- Demonstrated competence
- Accurate testing methods
- Proper equipment
- Reliable results
- Internationally recognised

**Practical Impact:**
COAs from ISO 17025 labs are more trustworthy.

### For CBD Companies (ISO 9001)

**If a Company Has ISO 9001:**
- Quality management system in place
- Documented procedures
- Consistent processes
- Commitment to improvement

**Practical Impact:**
Better confidence in overall company operations.

### For Manufacturers (ISO 22000)

**If a Manufacturer Has ISO 22000:**
- Food safety prioritised
- Hazard controls in place
- Safe production practices
- Traceability systems

**Practical Impact:**
Reduced contamination risk.

## How to Verify ISO Certification

### Checking Validity

**Steps:**
1. Ask for certificate
2. Note certification body
3. Check certificate number
4. Verify with certification body
5. Confirm scope covers relevant activities

### What to Look For

**Legitimate Certificates Show:**
- Certification body name
- Certificate number
- Scope of certification
- Expiry date
- Accreditation marks

### Red Flags

**Suspicious If:**
- Can't provide certificate
- Vague claims ("ISO compliant")
- Unknown certification body
- Expired certificates
- Can't verify with issuer

## ISO Certification Process

### How Companies Get Certified

**General Process:**
1. Implement required systems
2. Document procedures
3. Apply to certification body
4. Undergo audit
5. Address any findings
6. Receive certification
7. Maintain through surveillance audits

### Ongoing Requirements

- Annual surveillance audits
- Full re-certification (usually every 3 years)
- Continuous compliance
- Documentation maintenance

## Does ISO Guarantee Quality?

### What It Does

**ISO Certification Shows:**
- Systems are in place
- Processes are documented
- External verification occurred
- Commitment to standards

### What It Doesn't Do

**ISO Doesn't Guarantee:**
- Every product is perfect
- No problems will ever occur
- Better than all non-certified
- Automatic quality

### Perspective

ISO is a strong indicator but not absolute assurance. It's one piece of the quality puzzle.

## What If No ISO?

### Not Necessarily Bad

**Consider:**
- Small companies may not have it
- Cost can be prohibitive
- Other quality indicators exist
- GMP may be present instead

### Minimum Standards

**At Minimum Look For:**
- Third-party testing (any accredited lab)
- Quality controls mentioned
- Transparent practices
- Good track record

---

## ISO Claims to Verify

### Common Claims

**"ISO Certified Lab Testing"**
- Lab should be ISO 17025 accredited
- Verify with lab directly

**"ISO 9001 Certified"**
- Ask for certificate
- Check it covers CBD operations
- Verify currency

**"ISO Compliant"**
- Weaker claim than "certified"
- May mean following standards without certification
- Ask for clarification
