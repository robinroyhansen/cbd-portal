# The Science of CBD: How Cannabidiol Works

By Robin Roy Krigslund-Hansen | 12+ years in CBD industry
Last updated: January 21, 2026

---

## Quick Summary

CBD (cannabidiol) is a non-intoxicating compound from cannabis that interacts with the body's endocannabinoid system and multiple other receptor systems. Unlike THC, CBD doesn't directly activate CB1 receptors, explaining why it doesn't cause intoxication while still producing therapeutic effects.

---

## Key Takeaways

- CBD interacts with over 65 molecular targets in the body
- Works through endocannabinoid system modulation, not direct CB1 activation
- Affects serotonin, GABA, and opioid receptor systems
- Has anti-inflammatory, anxiolytic, and neuroprotective properties
- Effects are typically subtle and build with consistent use

---

## CBD's Molecular Mechanisms

### Endocannabinoid System Modulation

CBD's primary mechanism involves the endocannabinoid system (ECS):

**CB1 Receptors:**
- CBD doesn't directly activate CB1 (unlike THC)
- Acts as a negative allosteric modulator
- May reduce THC's effects at CB1
- Explains non-intoxicating nature

**CB2 Receptors:**
- Weak inverse agonist at CB2
- May modulate immune function
- Influences inflammatory responses

**Endocannabinoid Enhancement:**
- Inhibits FAAH (enzyme breaking down anandamide)
- Increases natural endocannabinoid levels
- Supports endocannabinoid tone

### Serotonin System (5-HT)

CBD significantly affects serotonin signalling:

- **5-HT1A agonist** — Direct activation of anxiety-reducing receptor
- May explain anxiolytic effects
- Similar mechanism to some antidepressants
- Contributes to anti-nausea effects

### GABA and Glutamate

CBD modulates the brain's excitatory/inhibitory balance:

- Enhances GABA signalling (calming neurotransmitter)
- Modulates glutamate (excitatory neurotransmitter)
- May explain anticonvulsant properties
- Contributes to anxiolytic effects

### Additional Targets

CBD interacts with numerous other systems:

- **TRPV1 receptors** — Pain and temperature perception
- **GPR55** — Potential "third" cannabinoid receptor
- **PPARγ** — Metabolism and inflammation
- **Adenosine receptors** — Sleep and relaxation
- **Opioid receptors** — Pain modulation

## CBD's Pharmacology

### Absorption and Bioavailability

How CBD enters the body varies by method:

| Method | Bioavailability | Onset | Duration |
|--------|----------------|-------|----------|
| Sublingual | 20-35% | 15-45 min | 4-6 hours |
| Oral | 6-19% | 1-2 hours | 6-8 hours |
| Inhalation | 30-40% | Minutes | 2-3 hours |
| Topical | Variable (local) | 15-45 min | 2-4 hours |

### Metabolism

CBD is processed primarily by the liver:

- CYP3A4 and CYP2C19 enzymes
- First-pass metabolism reduces oral bioavailability
- Fat-soluble, stored in adipose tissue
- Half-life: 18-32 hours with regular use

### Drug Interactions

CBD's enzyme interactions can affect other medications:

- Inhibits CYP2D6, CYP2C9, and CYP2C19
- May increase levels of medications using these pathways
- "Grapefruit warning" medications particularly relevant
- Always consult healthcare providers

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Why doesn't CBD get you high like THC?

THC directly activates CB1 receptors in the brain, producing intoxication. CBD doesn't activate CB1—it actually modulates it negatively, potentially reducing THC's effects. CBD works through other systems that don't produce intoxication.

### Does CBD work immediately?

Acute effects (like relaxation) may be noticeable relatively quickly with some delivery methods. However, many of CBD's benefits require consistent use over weeks as it builds in the system and modulates endocannabinoid tone.

### How is CBD different from pharmaceuticals targeting the same receptors?

CBD typically has gentler, more subtle effects because it modulates receptors rather than fully activating or blocking them. This produces fewer side effects but also less dramatic acute effects than pharmaceuticals.

## My Take
