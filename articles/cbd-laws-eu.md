# CBD Laws in the European Union: A Complete Guide

By Robin Roy Krigslund-Hansen | 12+ years in CBD industry
Last updated: January 21, 2026

---

## Quick Summary

CBD laws in the European Union have evolved significantly, with the landmark 2020 European Court of Justice ruling confirming CBD is not a narcotic. While EU-wide Novel Food regulations govern CBD as a food supplement, individual member states maintain varying approaches to THC limits, product types, and enforcement. Understanding both EU-level rules and country-specific regulations is essential for consumers and businesses.

---

## Key Takeaways

- CBD ruled not a narcotic by EU Court (2020)
- Novel Food regulation applies EU-wide
- THC limits vary by country (0.2-0.3%)
- Member states have different approaches
- Regulations still evolving

## EU-Level CBD Regulation

### The Kanavape Ruling (2020)

**Landmark Decision:**
- European Court of Justice case
- November 2020 ruling
- CBD cannot be considered a narcotic
- Free movement of goods applies
- Set precedent for CBD trade

**Significance:**
- CBD products can move between EU states
- National bans on CBD became harder to justify
- Legitimised CBD industry in Europe
- Didn't override all national rules

### Novel Food Regulation

**What It Means:**
- CBD extracts classified as Novel Food
- Requires pre-market authorisation
- Food Safety Authority assessment
- Currently many applications pending

**Current Status:**
- Applications submitted by companies
- Assessment ongoing
- Products remain on market (enforcement varies)
- Full authorisation expected eventually

### EU Hemp THC Limit

**Legal Hemp Cultivation:**
- 0.3% THC (increased from 0.2% in 2023)
- Applies to agricultural hemp
- Certified seed varieties
- Different from product THC limits

## Country-by-Country Overview

### Germany

**Status: Generally Permitted**

**Key Points:**
- CBD products widely available
- Novel Food rules apply
- 0.2% THC limit for products
- Pharmacies sell CBD
- Food products under scrutiny

**Enforcement:**
- Relatively permissive
- Large market

### France

**Status: Previously Restrictive, Now Opening**

**Key Points:**
- Raw flowers were banned (overturned)
- CBD extracts permitted
- No THC (0.0%) in finished products historically
- Courts ruling against restrictions
- Market expanding

**Recent Changes:**
- Flower ban declared illegal
- Aligning with EU rulings

### Italy

**Status: Complex**

**Key Points:**
- "Cannabis light" widely sold
- 0.5-0.6% THC threshold debated
- Legal grey areas remain
- Large market despite uncertainty
- Regional variations

### Spain

**Status: Topical Only (Officially)**

**Key Points:**
- Topical CBD products permitted
- Oral consumption technically not authorised
- "Cannabis clubs" operate
- Cosmetic classification common
- Enforcement varies

### Netherlands

**Status: Somewhat Restrictive**

**Key Points:**
- Surprisingly strict for Netherlands
- Food-based CBD products limited
- Topicals permitted
- Pharmacy products available
- Novel Food strictly enforced

### Austria

**Status: Restrictive**

**Key Points:**
- Non-food CBD products only
- Tobacco shops sell CBD
- Cosmetics permitted
- Food products restricted
- Conservative approach

### Belgium

**Status: Restrictive**

**Key Points:**
- Novel Food strictly applied
- CBD food products limited
- Cosmetics permitted
- 0.2% THC limit
- Conservative enforcement

### Poland

**Status: Generally Permitted**

**Key Points:**
- Growing market
- 0.2% THC limit
- Food and supplement products
- Relatively open approach
- Eastern Europe hub

### Czech Republic

**Status: Permissive**

**Key Points:**
- Liberal approach
- 0.3% THC limit
- Wide product availability
- Established market
- Progressive regulations

### Switzerland (Non-EU)

**Status: Very Permissive**

**Key Points:**
- 1% THC allowed (much higher than EU)
- Wide product variety
- Cigarette-style CBD products
- Not bound by EU Novel Food
- Distinct market

## Novel Food Status

### What Novel Food Means

**Definition:**
- Foods not consumed significantly before 1997
- Require safety assessment
- Must be authorised for sale
- EU-wide regulation

### CBD and Novel Food

**Current Situation:**
- CBD extracts classified as Novel Food
- Synthetic CBD also Novel Food
- Applications pending
- Products still sold pending decisions

**Practical Impact:**
- Businesses applying for authorisation
- Products remain available (mostly)
- Enforcement varies by country
- Long-term compliance required

## Common Questions

### "Is CBD legal in the EU?"

**Answer:** Generally yes, but with caveats. CBD itself is not considered a narcotic. However, product regulations vary by country, and Novel Food rules apply to CBD food products.

### "Can I travel with CBD in Europe?"

**Answer:** Generally yes for personal use within the EU. Keep products in original packaging with visible THC content. Check specific country rules for destinations.

### "Which EU country has the best CBD laws?"

**Answer:** Czech Republic, Germany, and Poland are relatively permissive. Switzerland (non-EU) has the most liberal approach with 1% THC allowed.

### "Will EU CBD laws become uniform?"

**Answer:** Likely over time. Novel Food authorisations will create some uniformity. However, member states will probably retain some variation.

## My Take

EU CBD law is a patchwork—the 2020 ECJ ruling was a watershed moment confirming CBD isn't a narcotic, but day-to-day reality still varies by country. Germany, Poland, and Czech Republic are good markets for consumers; France and Austria are more restrictive. The Novel Food situation is the big uncertainty—many companies have applied, but full authorisation takes time. For consumers, the practical reality is that CBD products are widely available throughout most of the EU, despite the regulatory complexity. Buy from reputable sources, check THC levels, and you'll generally be fine. The direction is clearly toward more acceptance, not less.
