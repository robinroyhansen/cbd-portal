# Cannabis Taxonomy: Species, Strains, and Cultivars Explained

By Robin Roy Krigslund-Hansen | 12+ years in CBD industry
Last updated: January 21, 2026

---

## Quick Summary

Cannabis taxonomy is debated among scientists. The genus *Cannabis* contains either one highly variable species (*C. sativa*) or multiple species (*C. sativa*, *C. indica*, *C. ruderalis*). Modern "strains" (properly called cultivars) are complex hybrids that don't map neatly onto species distinctions. The indica/sativa labels used by consumers describe effects, not genetics.

---

## The Scientific Classification

### Kingdom to Genus
- Kingdom: Plantae
- Phylum: Magnoliophyta (flowering plants)
- Class: Magnoliopsida
- Order: Rosales
- Family: Cannabaceae
- Genus: *Cannabis*

### The Species Debate

**One-species view (lumpers):**
All cannabis is *Cannabis sativa L.* with subspecies/varieties:
- *C. sativa* subsp. *sativa*
- *C. sativa* subsp. *indica*
- *C. sativa* var. *ruderalis*

**Multiple-species view (splitters):**
- *Cannabis sativa* (narrow-leaf, equatorial)
- *Cannabis indica* (broad-leaf, mountain)
- *Cannabis ruderalis* (wild, auto-flowering)

**Scientific consensus:** Leans toward one variable species, but debate continues.

## Historical Species Descriptions

### Cannabis sativa (Linnaeus, 1753)
- First formally named
- Tall, narrow leaves
- Longer flowering time
- Equatorial origins
- Traditional fiber hemp

### Cannabis indica (Lamarck, 1785)
- Named from Indian specimens
- Shorter, bushier
- Broader leaves
- Shorter flowering time
- Mountain/Afghan origins

### Cannabis ruderalis (Janischewsky, 1924)
- Wild/feral cannabis
- Auto-flowering (not light-dependent)
- Low THC
- Central Asian origin
- Rarely cultivated pure

## Modern Understanding

### Why Simple Categories Don't Work

After 50+ years of breeding:
- Most cannabis is highly hybridized
- Pure "sativa" or "indica" rare
- Genetics don't match folk categories
- Chemical profile matters more than appearance

### Chemovars vs. Cultivars

**Cultivar (cultivated variety):**
- Named variety bred for specific traits
- "Blue Dream," "Charlotte's Web," etc.
- What we mean by "strains"

**Chemovar (chemical variety):**
- Classified by chemical profile
- Type I (THC-dominant), Type II (balanced), Type III (CBD-dominant)
- More scientifically useful

## The Indica/Sativa Consumer Myth

### What People Think
- Sativa = energizing, uplifting
- Indica = sedating, relaxing
- This guides consumer choices

### Scientific Reality
- Effect labels don't correlate with genetics
- Modern strains are complex hybrids
- Terpene profiles may explain effects better
- Myrcene content (not genetics) linked to sedation

### Why the Myth Persists
- Simple, memorable categories
- Some loose correlation with original landrace effects
- Industry uses it for marketing
- Consumers find it helpful despite inaccuracy

## Landrace Strains

### What They Are
- Original, pure regional varieties
- Adapted to specific environments
- Minimal modern breeding
- Named after origin (Afghan, Thai, etc.)

### Examples
| Landrace | Region | Typical Profile |
|----------|--------|-----------------|
| Afghan | Afghanistan | Short, broad-leaf, high THC |
| Thai | Thailand | Tall, narrow-leaf, long flowering |
| Durban Poison | South Africa | Energizing, sweet |
| Hindu Kush | Pakistan | Sedating, earthy |
| Acapulco Gold | Mexico | Uplifting, golden |

### Conservation Concern
- Many landraces disappearing
- Hybridization diluting genetics
- Seed banks preserving some
- Cultural heritage aspect

## Hemp vs. Drug Cannabis

### Not Species Difference
- Same plant, different breeding
- Hemp bred for fiber/seeds/CBD
- Drug cannabis bred for THC
- Legally defined by THC content (<0.3% = hemp)

### Breeding History
- Separate for centuries
- Different selection pressures
- Industrial vs. psychoactive use
- Recently converging (CBD hemp)

---

## Modern Breeding

### Goals
- High cannabinoid content
- Specific terpene profiles
- Disease resistance
- Yield optimization
- Consistent effects

### Techniques
- Traditional selective breeding
- Feminized seeds
- Autoflowering hybrids
- Some genetic modification (controversial)

---

## Genetic Testing

### Current Capabilities
- Can distinguish hemp from drug cannabis
- Can identify some cultivar genetics
- Chemotype prediction improving
- Parentage verification possible

### Limitations
- Genetic diversity within "strains"
- Same name ≠ same genetics
- Labels often unreliable
- Testing not standardized

---

## Why Taxonomy Matters for CBD

### Quality and Consistency
- Knowing the cultivar helps predict profile
- Genetic stability affects consistency
- Breeding for specific cannabinoids

### Legal Compliance
- Hemp must be <0.3% THC
- Genetics affect THC potential
- Certified hemp seed important
- Varietal registration systems

## My Take

The indica/sativa distinction has become like the term "organic" in food—meaningful to consumers but often misleading. I've seen "indica" products that are energizing and "sativa" products that knock you out. Chemical profile (cannabinoids + terpenes) predicts effects far better than these labels.

For CBD consumers specifically, the species question matters less than the chemovar. What matters is: CBD-dominant plant, well-characterized terpene profile, consistent genetics. Whether you call the source plant "hemp," "sativa," or "cannabis" is less important than what's actually in the product.
